eagle – schematic

  1. Eagle has a verb-noun working flow, each time you select a verb (i.e., move, add, copy) and click the object. Group operation is therefore, group > move > right click > group move. Commands can be abbrievated, move can be typed in the command line as mov, copy is cop, etc. Each time you add a component, there is a mirror icon just beside the grid icon.

  2. You may move component to connect to another pin, and to visually connect a wire to a pin, or cross wires, you may add a junction to the crosspoint. Airwires are thin lines that have not been routed yet.

eagle – PCB layout

usually, you may route with 10mil width and 8mil spacing, try to see how far you can go

  1. While you in move command, press Ctrl key while clicking a component, the origin of the component will snap to the grid. You must decide whether you route in the top or bottom layer. In surface mount devices, we usually route on the top copper layer. When you placed all the components, you may use ratsnest to recalculate the air wires. If you want to use the autorouter, be sure to setup the grid to appropriate width, for example, 7mil or 14mil, and then press the autorouter, set the line width to the corresponding value.

  2. Routing the GND plane. This is most important when you are using eagle. First, select the polygon tool, change line width to 10mil, and select on (the icon with a small circle on the up-left corner) to isolate parts and wires, the isolation width is 12mil, spacing (between the ground plane and the boarder of the PCB). Second, draw a rectangular around the board. Third, name the polygon GND. Final, press the ratsnest to generate the GND plane. Note, when you re-open the PCB board, the copper pour is not shown, so you need to press ratsnest to regenerate the GND plane. Try, always pour GND plane with 10mil isolation. You could modify the isolation and spacing of GND polygon by (i) information button.

  3. Some useful .ulp scripts by sparkfun is silk_gen.ulp, which will process fancy bSilk and tSilk layers. Some important information, such as polarize of diode or capacitors, can be shown in the silk layer. (WARNING, you may simply add text to tPlace layer). Next, make sure you add text to your functional pins, and place them on the tPlace layer, please use the change button to alter the size of text to 0.05". You may use the eye button to show the connection of pins and wires. Add time stamp to the tBottom layer where there is no connections, this will automatically mirro the text, it's cool.

  4. The 0.05" grid rule (found within the Eagle doc) applies to the component centers. Many component footprints are hard metric (for example 0.5mm spacing or 1x1.2mm pad sizes) and it is completely ok to layout pads and parts on the mm grid. However, when placing components I recommend the component centers stay ‘on grid’. This will help with routing and overall component alignment.

  5. Hand route critical or sensitive parts of certain boards, Spend your time innovating, not routing mundane traces. Remember, add (1) Date code, (2) Silkscreen title and pin labels, (3) Standoff holes drews, (4) All connections routed, (5) TX and RX routed correctly, you may name RX-I and TX-O. (6) add debug vias, leds, and pullup and pulldown resistors.

Analog PCB design rules (by Ondo88)

  1. Some PCB software offer “tear” connections for pads and vias. This reduces mechanical stress around the via connection, avoiding possible rupture of the traces. Again, it’s also useful for RF applications, as it makes a better Z matching (it behaves as a tamper used in micro-wave circuits).

  2. It’s a good practice to place holes around the boards, joining top and bottom ground layers. That makes the board more similar to a Faraday cage, reducing greatly effects from EMI (Electro-magnetic Interference). But to be truth, randomizing the distance between the holes makes a better isolation as no single frequency gets benefited form constructive interference.

  3. Get to know what you’re building. Before getting serious about PCB design, you should learn what is everything you’re using on them. I’ve always said you can know if someone is a good PCB designer by asking him to give you FR4 permitivity, solder fusion temperature and the like (without checking wikipedia ;D).

  4. Another common mistake is not taking account of thermal activity on the board. Make sure your temperature sensitive components (like oscillators, and almost any analog circuit) are far from your heat generating circuits (regulators and the like). If your board fits in a box, be sure to have cooling paths that keep the things ok.

  5. Some chips may have two Vcc or GND pins one to each other. On SMD packages it’s tempting to just join them straight, avoid that if you’re using oven soldering (very likely on production). That will unbalance the thermal forces your chip may suffer, moving it from its original position, and possibly making it tombstone.

  6. I’ve found very useful to place a couple of squares on silkscreen, with an E (Error) and a T (Tested) on the side of each one. That allows you to mark easily your boards with anything you may have at hand, from a pen to a screwdriver.

  7. The last one is the more ethereal of the advises, “follow your ground paths”. Your signal propagates from your Vcc to your GND, mainly through the shortest path, if two signals share that ground path, they’ll interfere each other. Try to make that paths as short and trouble free as you can.

eagle – Gerber viewer

Gerber Files are txt files with coordinates that tell the PCB machines to go to location X,Y and do something (drill, expose, etch, print, etc). Because there are different layers to your PCB, you need to create different text files for the different layers. This is where the Eagle CAM program comes into play.

Here, we will use sfe-gerb274x.cam script by sparkfun. Remember Drill file is 2:4 Leading. Anytime you transmit a PCB layout to a fab house, you need to pass them 7 files, and 7 files only:

  1. boardname.GTL Top Copper Layer

  2. boardname.GBL Bottom Copper Layer

  3. boardname.GTS Top Soldermask

  4. boardname.GBS Bottom Soldermask

  5. boardname.GTO Top Silkscreen

  6. boardname.GBO Bottom Silkscreen

  7. boardname.GKO Board Outline

  8. boardname.G2L only if you're uploading a four layer board

  9. boardname.G3L only if you're uploading a four layer board

  10. boardname.XLN Drills (2:4 Leading – remember this)

You will also see a GTP file extension. This is the Gerber Top Paste file. GTP includes all the paste for the pads of SMD and GTS includes both pads and vias.

Last, but not finally, you can use kicad to visualize the gerber file.
